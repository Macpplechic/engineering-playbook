# How I Give Feedback

Feedback is not a gift I give you at the end of the year. It's part of how I work with you every day.

## My approach

**In real time.** If I see something worth noting — positive or corrective — I'll say it close to when it happened. Not weeks later in a review. Delayed feedback loses its usefulness.

**Direct and specific.** I don't do vague. "Good job" tells you nothing. "The way you handled that incident call — you kept the room calm and got to the root cause in under 20 minutes — that's exactly the kind of leadership I want more of" tells you something.

**Focused on behavior, not character.** I'll tell you what I observed and what the impact was. I won't tell you who you are. You're not "difficult" — you interrupted three people in that meeting and it shut down the conversation. Those are different things.

## What I expect from you

Take it. Sit with it. Don't immediately defend — listen first. You don't have to agree, but you do have to engage.

And give it back. Tell me when I'm wrong. Tell me when something I'm doing isn't working for you. I need feedback too, and I take it seriously when someone has the courage to give it.

## On performance reviews

Reviews should never be a surprise. If something shows up in a formal review that you're hearing for the first time, I've failed you. My goal is that by the time we get to any formal process, we've already had every hard conversation that needs to be had.
